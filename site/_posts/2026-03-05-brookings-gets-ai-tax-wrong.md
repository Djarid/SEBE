In January, the Brookings Institution published a paper on how to tax an automated economy. Anton Korinek (who sits on Anthropic's Economic Advisory Council) and Lee Lockwood correctly identify the problem: AI will erode the labour income that funds most government revenue. 75% of US federal tax comes from labour. In the UK, over 42% of receipts are employment-linked. As automation scales, this base disappears.

Their diagnosis is right. Their prescription is to shift the tax burden onto consumption while protecting AI capital from all taxation. They compare compute taxes to "taxing steel during the industrial revolution." They propose sovereign wealth funds and voluntary corporate "windfall clauses" as fiscal insurance.

This is wrong. Here is why.

## Consumption tax eats itself

A consumption tax requires consumers with money. If automation displaces workers, those workers earn less and spend less. Consumption tax receipts fall. The revenue base erodes in lockstep with the crisis it is supposed to address.

The authors know this. They acknowledge that payroll tax revenue will decline "just as needs for retraining programs and transition support increase." Their solution is to tax the reduced spending of the people who just lost their jobs. This is not a bridge. It is a plank.

Consumption tax is also regressive. This is not controversial. Low earners spend a higher share of income than high earners. The paper proposes offsetting this by taxing the difference between income and net savings at progressive rates, which requires knowing everyone's income and savings, which requires the same administrative apparatus as income tax. They have rebuilt income taxation inside consumption taxation and called it innovation.

## "Don't tax capital" is a category error

The paper warns against taxing AI infrastructure. Robot taxes and compute taxes discourage investment, they argue. Fair enough, as far as robot taxes go. But the Sovereign Energy and Bandwidth Excise (SEBE) is not a capital tax. It does not tax owning servers. It taxes the electricity they consume.

A company that replaces 100 workers with 1 efficient server pays less SEBE than a company running a legacy data centre at twice the wattage. The incentive is to invest in better hardware, not to avoid investing. Their steel analogy is wrong. SEBE does not tax the steel. It taxes the coal that fires the furnace. The result is better furnaces.

## Voluntary commitments are not fiscal policy

The paper proposes "windfall clauses" where AI companies voluntarily share gains if they achieve breakthroughs. OpenAI restructured from non-profit to capped-profit to for-profit in under five years. Voluntary commitments last exactly as long as they remain convenient.

Sovereign wealth funds are a serious idea, but they need revenue to capitalise them. Norway's fund runs on petroleum taxation. You need a tax mechanism first. The Brookings paper has no mechanism. It has a direction of travel.

## The category they cannot see

Korinek and Lockwood offer a binary: tax consumption or tax capital. SEBE does neither. It taxes the physical inputs to production (energy and cross-border data) at infrastructure that already exists (power meters, internet exchange points). It is not a capital tax because it does not penalise ownership. It is not a consumption tax because it does not depend on downstream spending. It sits in a category the paper's framework does not contain, which is why they cannot find it.

SEBE generates an estimated £34-46 billion at launch in 2030 (2026 prices), growing to £93 billion by 2040 as automation scales. The [revenue model]({{ site.baseurl }}/docs/revenue_model.html) is published with rates, tiers, source data and methodology. The consumption tax alternative has no revenue estimate, no rate structure and no growth model.

## The question they asked, the answer they missed

The Brookings paper asks: how do you fund government when labour taxation fails? The answer is not to tax the people who lost their jobs on what little they can still afford to buy. The answer is to tax the infrastructure that replaced them, at the physical layer, where measurement is objective, evasion is difficult and revenue grows automatically with the problem.

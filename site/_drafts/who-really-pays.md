The most common objection to taxing automation infrastructure is that it will raise prices. Tax production inputs, the argument goes, and companies pass the cost to consumers. Low earners spend a higher proportion of income on consumption. Therefore the tax is regressive.

This is half an argument. Here is the other half.

## The existing system is already regressive

The UK raises 42% of government revenue from Income Tax and National Insurance. Both are levied on earned income from employment. The assumption is that this is progressive because the rates are graduated: higher earners pay higher percentages.

The assumption is wrong. Income tax only taxes people who work for wages. The wealthiest people in Britain do not work for wages. They derive income from capital gains, dividends, inheritance, trust distributions, and corporate structures. Capital gains are taxed at lower rates than employment income. Dividends carry separate (lower) allowances. Inheritance is routinely structured through trusts and business property relief. Corporate retained earnings are not taxed as personal income at all until distributed, and distribution can be deferred indefinitely or routed through jurisdictions with favourable treaty arrangements.

The result: the people actually funding public services through income tax are working and middle-income earners. Not the wealthiest. The burden falls on those least able to avoid it. This is regressive in practice regardless of what the rate card says.

VAT is worse. It taxes consumption. A household spending 100% of its income (because it has no choice) pays VAT on all of it. A wealthy household saving or investing 80% of income pays VAT on 20%. The wealthy also have access to VAT-exempt financial instruments, corporate purchasing structures, and offshore procurement. A billionaire's effective VAT rate as a proportion of total income is negligible. A minimum-wage worker's is not.

These are not radical observations. They are established public finance. The UK tax system is nominally progressive and functionally regressive. The people with the least pay the most as a proportion of what they have.

## The SEBE difference

The Sovereign Energy and Bandwidth Excise taxes commercial electricity consumption at facilities above 500kW and commercial data crossing the UK digital border. The incidence is on large-scale automation infrastructure: data centres, automated warehouses, heavy industrial, hyperscale compute.

This is structurally different from income tax and VAT in three ways.

**First, the collection base is corporate infrastructure, not individual activity.** Income tax requires a worker earning a wage. VAT requires a consumer buying a product. SEBE requires a facility consuming electricity above a commercial threshold. There is no individual at the collection point. The liability falls on the entity operating the infrastructure.

**Second, the avoidance mechanisms do not exist.** A wealthy individual avoids income tax by restructuring income as capital gains. They avoid VAT by reducing visible consumption or routing purchases through corporate vehicles. There is no equivalent manoeuvre for SEBE. A data centre cannot restructure its electricity consumption as a capital gain. It cannot route its kilowatt-hours through a subsidiary in Luxembourg. The meter reads what it reads. You cannot transfer-price a unit of energy.

**Third, the threshold exempts the entire consumer economy.** The 500kW floor means every small business, every shop, every restaurant, every household pays nothing. SEBE applies only to large-scale commercial operations. There is no mechanism by which a corner shop or a low-income household receives a SEBE bill. The tax physically cannot reach them at the collection point.

## The pass-through question

The legitimate version of the regressivity critique is about pass-through. Companies paying SEBE may raise prices to maintain margins. Those price increases reach consumers. Low-income consumers are hit hardest proportionally.

This is real but overstated for SEBE specifically, and irrelevant without examining the distribution side.

The pass-through chain for SEBE is long and indirect. A data centre operator pays SEE. That cost is one component of the price charged to a cloud provider. The cloud provider's cost is one component of the price charged to a SaaS company. The SaaS company's cost is one component of the operating expenses of a business using the software. That business may or may not pass the cost to its customers. At each link, competitive pressure, margin absorption, and efficiency gains dilute the pass-through. The relationship between SEE paid at a data centre meter and the price of a loaf of bread is real but attenuated.

More importantly, pass-through analysis without distribution analysis is the same half-argument. Every tax has a collection side and a distribution side. Progressivity is the net of both.

## The distribution side

If SEBE revenue funds Universal Basic Income, the net transfer is from corporate energy consumers to every adult equally. A flat payment of any amount is proportionally more valuable to a low earner than a high earner. £650 per year is meaningful to someone on £15,000. It is negligible to someone on £150,000. The collection falls on concentrated corporate infrastructure. The distribution is universal. The net effect is progressive by definition.

If SEBE revenue funds infrastructure investment (public transport, broadband, energy grid, NHS capital), the beneficiaries are disproportionately lower-income households who depend on public services and cannot buy private alternatives. The net effect is progressive.

If SEBE revenue funds National Insurance reductions, the benefit goes directly to workers as reduced employment cost. Progressive.

The only scenario in which SEBE produces a regressive net outcome is if the revenue is used to cut taxes that primarily benefit the wealthy. That is a distribution choice, not a property of SEBE.

## The renewable exemption trap

A recently proposed alternative to SEBE suggests a compute infrastructure levy with a renewable energy exemption: if the operator funds new renewable generation sufficient to power the facility, the levy disappears.

This sounds progressive. It is regressive.

Microsoft, Google, and Amazon can afford to build wind farms. A mid-sized UK data centre operator cannot. The exemption creates a two-tier system: hyperscalers with deep capital reserves escape the levy entirely, while smaller domestic operators (who cannot finance renewable generation at scale) pay the full rate. The richest companies pay nothing. The less wealthy pay everything. This is the definition of regressive.

SEBE avoids this by taxing energy consumption regardless of source. A data centre powered by renewables pays the same SEE rate as one powered by gas. The Pigouvian benefit (energy efficiency incentive) still operates: more efficient facilities consume less energy and pay less tax. But there is no escape hatch for those with the deepest pockets.

There is a second problem with the renewable exemption. A green data centre consuming 50MW of renewable energy is still displacing workers. The energy source does not change the automation externality. The fiscal function of SEBE (withdrawing purchasing power from the corporate sector, funding redistribution) matters regardless of how the electrons are generated. An exemption based on energy source addresses the environmental question but ignores the economic one entirely.

## The carbon tax comparison

Carbon tax faces the same regressivity critique. Higher energy prices hit low-income households hardest. The answer, implemented in Canada and proposed elsewhere, is the carbon dividend: collect the tax, redistribute it universally. The net effect is progressive because high emitters pay more than they receive and low emitters receive more than they pay.

SEBE follows the same logic but with a critical advantage. Carbon tax is designed to shrink its own base. If it works (emissions fall), revenue falls. The progressive redistribution capacity diminishes over time. Success undermines the fiscal mechanism.

SEBE's base grows. As automation expands, energy consumption at large facilities increases. Revenue grows with the thing it taxes. The progressive redistribution capacity increases over time. The longer SEBE operates, the more it can redistribute. This is the opposite of the carbon tax trajectory: SEBE becomes more progressive as the economy automates, not less.

## The real question

The regressivity critique of SEBE is a question about distribution, not collection. SEBE's collection mechanism is more progressive than income tax (which exempts the wealthy) and more progressive than VAT (which hits the poorest hardest). Its distribution mechanism is a political choice: UBI, infrastructure, NI reduction, or some combination.

The existing UK tax system taxes workers and consumers to fund public services while the wealthiest use legal structures to contribute proportionally less. SEBE taxes corporate automation infrastructure with no avoidance mechanism and no individual at the collection point.

The question is not whether SEBE is regressive. The question is why we tolerate a system that already is.

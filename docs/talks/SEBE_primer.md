# SECTION 2: The Mechanism

> 🔵 **Check the clock. You should be at 2:00. You have 3 MINUTES for this section. Stop at 5:00.**

### One sentence:

> *"SEBE taxes the physical infrastructure of automated production: energy consumption and cross-border commercial data."*

### Two components:

**Component 1: Sovereign Energy Excise (SEE)**

- Taxes commercial electricity consumption above 500kW IT load.
- Every household exempt. Every small business exempt. Every shop, restaurant, school exempt.
- Who pays: data centres, automated warehouses, AI training facilities, large-scale manufacturing.
- Three-tier banded rate: £0.08/kWh (500kW–5MW), £0.20/kWh (5–50MW), £0.45/kWh (50MW+).
- Measured by Hardware Root of Trust meters at point of generation, storage, and load. You cannot game a kilowatt-hour.

> 🔴 **STOP — Do NOT explain the three-point metering architecture (PoG/PoS/PoL) unless asked. Do NOT mention BGP tagging, SNI filtering, or the National Telemetry API. That's for the Tech Working Group, not here.**

**Component 2: Digital Customs Duty (DCD)**

- Taxes cross-border commercial data throughput.
- Individual internet users exempt. Net neutrality preserved. Only commercial API traffic above sector baseline.
- Domestic data centres pay base rate. Offshore compute (AWS Ireland, Azure Netherlands) pays 2× rate.
- Effect: makes it cheaper to build and run data centres in the UK than to offshore them.

> 🔴 **STOP — Do NOT get into the Role Margin Protocol or sector SIC baselines. If asked, say "The technical spec is in the GitHub repo, happy to walk through it separately."**

### The killer point:

> *"You cannot transfer-price a kilowatt-hour to Dublin. The tax base is physical reality, not accounting opinion. That's why this works where corporation tax fails."*

# SECTION 3: The Revenue

> 🔵 **Check the clock. You should be at 5:00. You have 2 MINUTES for this section. Stop at 7:00.**

### Launch revenue (2030):

- **£34–46 billion per year** at launch. All figures in 2026 prices.
- For context: that's larger than Council Tax (£40bn) on day one.

### Growth trajectory:

- **£93 billion by 2040** — comparable to National Insurance.
- **£159 billion by 2045** — approaching Income Tax.
- Revenue grows AUTOMATICALLY as automation scales. No legislative action needed to increase it.

### The critical comparison with the carbon tax:

The party's current carbon tax proposal (£120/tonne rising to £500/tonne) is projected to raise £80bn/year.

**The problem:**

- It's a Pigouvian tax. If it works environmentally — if businesses decarbonise — the revenue disappears.
- You cannot simultaneously depend on £80bn/year from carbon AND expect it to eliminate carbon emissions.
- SEBE doesn't have this problem. When a data centre switches from gas to renewables, the carbon tax revenue vanishes but SEBE revenue stays exactly the same. The tax base is energy consumed, not fuel type.

**They're complementary:**

- Carbon tax provides the environmental incentive (Pigouvian).
- SEBE provides the fiscal foundation (permanent, growing).
- One fades as the other rises. That's a feature, not a bug.

# Q&A PREPARATION

These are the questions you'll get. Have the answers ready. **Keep each answer to 30 seconds maximum.**

---

## Q: "Isn't this regressive? Energy taxes hit everyone."

**Answer:**

- Consumers are completely exempt. SEBE only applies to commercial facilities above 500kW.
- Yes, costs will partially pass through to consumer prices. That's true of every business tax.
- But the revenue funds UBI — a universal transfer. A regressive tax funding a progressive transfer produces the most redistributive net outcome. That's the Scandinavian model.
- Judge the system in aggregate, not the tax in isolation.

---

## Q: "Won't companies just offshore their data centres?"

**Answer:**

- That's what the Digital Customs Duty prevents. Offshore compute pays 2× the domestic rate.
- It's cheaper to stay in the UK and pay SEE than to move abroad and pay DCD.
- And you can't offshore the energy consumption of a physical factory or warehouse. The productive infrastructure stays.

---

## Q: "How is this different from the carbon tax?"

**Answer:**

- Carbon tax only covers fossil fuel emissions. SEBE covers all energy, regardless of source.
- Carbon tax revenue disappears as decarbonisation succeeds. SEBE revenue grows as automation scales.
- They're complementary: carbon tax for environmental incentive, SEBE for fiscal sustainability.

---

## Q: "Where are the detailed costings?"

**Answer:**

- Full revenue model, sensitivity analysis, and technical specification are open-source at github.com/djarid/SEBE under CC-BY 4.0.
- It's been submitted to HM Treasury, the IPPR, and a summary shared with Richard Murphy at Tax Research UK at his request.
- What it needs now is formal economic modelling. I'm an infrastructure engineer, not an economist. I've built the mechanism — it needs institutional partners to stress-test the numbers.

---

## Q: "What about small businesses?"

**Answer:**

- Completely exempt. The 500kW threshold means only large commercial facilities pay.
- For reference, 500kW IT load is a significant data centre or large automated warehouse. Your local business, pub, or office is nowhere near this.
- Small businesses actually benefit: their customers have more money via UBI.

---

## Q: "Is this technically feasible? How do you measure it?"

**Answer:**

- Hardware Root of Trust metering at three points: generation, storage, and load. Same principle as existing smart meter infrastructure, scaled up.
- Energy consumption is the most objectively measurable thing in economics. A kilowatt-hour is a kilowatt-hour. No accountant can reclassify it.
- I've spent 30 years building and securing infrastructure, from military communications to large-scale enterprise automation. The technical spec is detailed in the proposal.

---

## Q: "What about the £200–500bn figure we've seen?"

**Answer:**

- The £200–500bn range is a long-term upper estimate from the academic brief, which includes aggressive rate assumptions and full-scale automation.
- The conservative, costed launch figure is £34–46bn. That's the number I'm asking you to evaluate.
- Revenue scales from there based on actual automation deployment, not projections.

---

## Q: "Why haven't other countries done this?"

**Answer:**

- Because the automation displacement hasn't been severe enough until now. AI changed the timeline.
- The UK has first-mover advantage: London is the world's largest financial technology hub, we have major data centre infrastructure, and we have a sovereign currency.
- Norway taxes energy extraction to fund its sovereign wealth fund. SEBE taxes energy consumption to fund universal income. Same principle, different application.

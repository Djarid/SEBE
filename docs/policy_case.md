# Taxing Automation Infrastructure

## The Policy Case for SEBE

**Author:** Jason Huxley
**Date:** March 2026
**Version:** 1.0
**Target:** Green Party Policy Development Committee / Economy Working Group

---

## 1. What Problem Does This Solve?

The UK government raises £534 billion per year (42% of all revenue) from
taxes on employment: Income Tax (£329B) and National Insurance (£205B).
Both depend on people being in paid work.

Automation is reducing the number of people in paid work. As it does so,
the revenue from these taxes falls. At the same time, displaced workers
increase welfare costs. Revenue shrinks; costs grow.

No existing UK tax grows with automation. Corporation Tax is structured
around profits, which multinational firms routinely shift offshore. VAT
depends on consumer spending, which falls with unemployment. Capital
Gains Tax is volatile. None of them track the physical infrastructure
that automates production.

This is the fiscal problem SEBE addresses: **the UK tax base is
structurally coupled to employment, and employment is declining**.

## 3. The Policy Question

The analysis above leads to a central question:

**If automation increases productive capacity while reducing employment,
how does the state maintain fiscal function?**

From an MMT perspective, a sovereign currency-issuing government is not
revenue-constrained in the way a household is. The UK government can
always spend in sterling. The real constraint is **inflation**: the
government can spend up to the point where demand exceeds the economy's
productive capacity.

Taxation in this framework serves three purposes:

1. **Withdrawing purchasing power** to control inflation (the primary
   macroeconomic function)
2. **Driving behaviour** (Pigouvian correction: discourage harms,
   encourage efficiency)
3. **Reducing inequality** (redistributive function)

SEBE performs all three:

- It **withdraws purchasing power from corporations** that benefit from
  automation, creating fiscal space for government spending without
  inflationary pressure
- It **incentivises energy efficiency** and **domestic compute
  investment** (Pigouvian function)
- It **generates revenue that can be redistributed**, reducing the
  inequality between capital owners and displaced workers

The MMT insight is that SEBE revenue does not "pay for" public spending
in a mechanical sense. Rather, it **expands the real fiscal space
available** by removing purchasing power from the corporate sector,
creating room for the government to spend (on UBI, public services, or
anything else) without exceeding productive capacity.

As automation increases productive capacity (more goods and services
produced per unit of input), the fiscal space expands further. SEBE
taxes the infrastructure of the very process that widens this space.
The tax and the productivity gain are structurally linked.

## 4. What Should the Policy Achieve?

Based on the harm/positive analysis:

### 4.1 Primary Objectives

1. **Replace eroding employment tax revenue** with a source that grows
   with automation, not against it
2. **Maintain aggregate demand** as employment declines, preventing
   deflationary spiral
3. **Ensure the gains from automation are distributed**, not
   concentrated in capital owners
4. **Create fiscal space** (in MMT terms) for the sovereign government
   to expand public provision as productive capacity grows

### 4.2 Secondary Objectives

5. **Incentivise energy efficiency** in compute and industrial
   operations
6. **Incentivise domestic compute infrastructure** (reducing dependency
   on offshore hyperscalers)
7. **Support the green transition** by making energy-intensive
   operations bear their social cost
8. **Preserve innovation incentives** (tax the resource consumed, not
   the output produced, so efficiency is rewarded)

### 4.3 Constraints

- Must not tax consumers or small businesses
- Must not require surveillance of individual behaviour
- Must be measurable through existing or near-existing infrastructure
- Must be harder to evade than existing corporate taxation
- Must not depend on international agreement to function unilaterally
- Must not hypothecate revenue (consistent with Green Party fiscal
  policy)

## 5. The Proposed Mechanism

The Sovereign Energy and Bandwidth Excise (SEBE) taxes two physical
inputs to automated production:

**1. Energy consumption (kWh)** at commercial facilities above a
threshold, through the Sovereign Energy Excise (SEE).

**2. Cross-border commercial data (TB)** at the UK digital border,
through the Digital Customs Duty (DCD).

### 5.1 Why These Two Inputs?

Every automated system, regardless of architecture, consumes energy.
A GPU, a TPU, an ASIC, a quantum processor, a robotic arm, an
automated warehouse: all require electricity. Energy consumption is
the universal physical signature of automation. It is already metered
at commercial scale. It cannot be faked without tampering with
hardware.

Cross-border data flow captures the value extracted by offshore
computation serving the UK market. A UK business using an AI model
hosted in Virginia generates no domestic energy consumption to tax,
but the data crosses the UK digital border and can be measured at
Internet Exchange Points. DCD ensures offshore automation cannot
escape the tax by operating from abroad.

### 5.2 Why Not Tax Compute Directly?

Proposals to tax FLOPS (floating-point operations), individual robots,
or AI models all fail on measurement. FLOPS is not a standardised unit
(FP64, FP32, FP16, INT8 all count differently). There is no physical
meter for compute operations. Evasion is architectural (switch hardware
type, change precision, use I/O-bound workloads). A FLOPS tax punishes
efficiency: a newer chip doing the same work in fewer operations pays
less.

Energy is architecture-neutral, physically measurable, tamper-resistant
and rewards efficiency. It is the correct proxy.

## 6. Revenue Estimate

All figures in 2026 real prices. All rates CPI-indexed annually.
Full derivation from first principles in `revenue_model.md`.

| Year | SEE (energy) | DCD (border data) | Total SEBE |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2030 (launch) | £30B | £8B | **£38B** |
| 2035 | £50B | £7B | **£57B** |
| 2040 | £83B | £10B | **£93B** |
| 2045 | £140B | £19B | **£159B** |

At launch, SEBE is comparable to Inheritance Tax + Stamp Duty + Climate
Change Levy + Tobacco Duty combined (~£34B). By 2040, it approaches
Corporation Tax scale (£100B). Revenue grows automatically because the
tax base (automation infrastructure) is the fastest-growing sector of
the economy.

SEBE is self-scaling: as machines replace workers, the tax on machine
infrastructure grows. This is the only UK tax with that property.

## 7. Possible Implementation Approaches

This section outlines approaches, not a fixed design. The detailed
engineering is a matter for expert consultation, legislative drafting
and pilot programmes. The policy sets the objective; implementation
follows.

### 7.1 Energy Component (SEE)

**Approach A: Existing commercial metering**
Use current half-hourly metering infrastructure already deployed at
large commercial sites. Advantages: no new hardware, fast rollout.
Limitations: does not capture private wire or self-generation.

**Approach B: Enhanced metering (Hardware Root of Trust)**
Install tamper-resistant meters at three points (generation, storage,
load) to close evasion routes. Advantages: prevents dark compute and
temporal arbitrage. Limitations: capital cost, 3-5 year rollout.

**Approach C: Phased hybrid**
Launch on existing metering (Approach A), mandate enhanced metering
for new connections and high-consumption sites, extend over time.

Any approach must ensure self-generated energy (on-site solar, private
wire, SMRs) is captured. The tax is on consumption, not on grid import.
A facility consuming 50 MW from a private gas turbine must be taxed
identically to one drawing 50 MW from the grid.

### 7.2 Border Data Component (DCD)

**Approach A: IXP-level measurement**
Measure commercial cross-border data flows at Internet Exchange Points
(LINX, LONAP, etc.) using existing peering infrastructure and BGP
routing data. Advantages: chokepoint measurement, no end-user
inspection. Limitations: requires ISP cooperation, commercial/personal
traffic classification.

**Approach B: Carrier-level reporting**
Require Tier-1 carriers to report aggregate commercial cross-border
throughput by customer. Advantages: leverages existing billing
infrastructure. Limitations: relies on carrier compliance.

**Approach C: Self-assessment with audit**
Businesses declare offshore compute usage; HMRC audits against ISP
records. Advantages: familiar compliance model. Limitations:
self-certification risk (the very problem SEBE should avoid).

### 7.3 Threshold and Rate Setting

The 500 kW threshold, tiered rates and specific values cited in
companion documents are worked examples based on current data. They are
a starting point for consultation, not fixed policy. The principles are:

- Exempt small businesses and consumers
- Progressive tiers (larger operations pay higher marginal rates)
- DCD rates set above SEE-equivalent cost (so offshoring is always
  more expensive than domestic operation)
- All rates CPI-indexed annually

Expert input from the energy sector, network engineering and tax
administration should inform the final rate structure.

## 8. What SEBE Does Not Do

This section addresses Paul Johnston's observation that the upside case
for automation should be considered alongside the harms.

**SEBE does not assume automation is bad.** It assumes that automation
shifts productive activity from a taxed base (employment) to an untaxed
one (infrastructure). The purchasing power that employment taxation
currently withdraws from the economy disappears as employment declines.
SEBE restores that withdrawal from a different source. It corrects the
imbalance, not the automation.

**SEBE does not discourage automation.** It taxes energy consumption,
not output or innovation. A company that automates efficiently (less
energy per unit of work) pays less SEE. The incentive is to automate
well, not to avoid automating.

**SEBE does not prevent AI-driven green transition.** Renewable-powered
data centres pay the same SEE rates but benefit from lower energy input
costs. The combination of SEE taxation and cheap renewables creates a
strong incentive to build green compute infrastructure in the UK. AI
research that accelerates the green transition is not penalised; it
withdraws the same purchasing power while delivering environmental
benefit.

**SEBE does not predetermine how the fiscal space is used.** The
purchasing power withdrawn by SEBE creates room for government net
spending. That spending could take the form of UBI (as proposed in
EC730), public services, infrastructure investment, or any combination.
The accompanying distribution model (`distribution_model.md`) is one
illustrative option, not a policy prescription. Consistent with Green
Party fiscal policy and MMT principles, SEBE revenue is not
hypothecated.

**SEBE is not contingent on worst-case automation scenarios.** Even if
AI development slows, if automation plateaus, or if the "robots take
all jobs" timeline extends by decades, SEBE still withdraws meaningful
purchasing power from the corporate sector (£15-19B at launch under
conservative assumptions, £45-50B by 2040). The fiscal space it creates
is useful regardless of how fast automation proceeds. If automation is
slower than projected, the need for that fiscal space is also smaller.
The mechanism scales with the problem in both directions.

## 9. The MMT Framework

For readers familiar with Modern Monetary Theory, SEBE fits naturally
within the MMT fiscal framework:

**Taxation as inflation control.** The UK government, as a sovereign
currency issuer, does not need tax revenue to "fund" spending. It needs
taxation to withdraw purchasing power from the private sector, creating
space for government spending without exceeding productive capacity.
SEBE withdraws £34-46B from corporate balance sheets at launch,
expanding the real fiscal space available for public provision.

**Productive capacity is the real constraint.** Automation increases
productive capacity. More goods and services can be produced with fewer
human inputs. This means the economy can absorb more government
spending before hitting the inflation wall. SEBE taxes the
infrastructure of the very process that expands this capacity.

**The sectoral balance.** In MMT terms, the government deficit is the
private sector surplus. If automation concentrates surplus in a small
number of capital owners (hyperscalers, tech monopolies), SEBE
redistributes that surplus more broadly, improving the sectoral balance
and sustaining aggregate demand.

**Automatic stabiliser.** SEBE revenue grows with automation. If
automation accelerates (more displacement, more fiscal need), SEBE
revenue grows in proportion. If automation slows, the fiscal need is
also reduced. The mechanism is self-calibrating in a way that
employment taxation is not.

**Not hypothecated.** Consistent with both MMT principles and Green
Party fiscal policy (EC661), SEBE revenue is not ring-fenced. The
government allocates it through normal democratic processes, responding
to the economic conditions of the day.

## 10. Addressing the Full Scenario Range

Paul Johnston posed the question: "Is it not possible that AI could
lead us into a world where computers do everything, nobody has to work,
and everything is fantastic?"

Yes. That is one possible outcome. But it requires a mechanism for
distributing the gains. If AI produces abundance but all the value
accrues to infrastructure owners, the result is plenty for the few and
destitution for the many. SEBE provides the distribution mechanism.

The scenario range, with SEBE's role in each:

**Scenario 1: Gradual automation, slow employment decline**
SEBE generates modest but growing revenue (£38B rising slowly). Funds
supplementary income or public service expansion. The tax base grows
steadily. No crisis, but the mechanism is in place when needed.

**Scenario 2: Rapid automation, significant employment displacement**
SEBE revenue grows quickly (£93-159B by 2040-2045). Funds meaningful
income replacement (UBI ramping to ULI). The fiscal gap from
employment tax loss is partially offset. The self-scaling property
is the critical feature.

**Scenario 3: AI-driven abundance (the optimistic case)**
Automation produces goods and services at near-zero marginal cost.
SEBE revenue is very large (energy consumption scales with output).
Productive capacity vastly exceeds demand. In MMT terms, the fiscal
space is enormous. SEBE provides the withdrawal mechanism that
prevents inflation while enabling universal distribution of abundance.
This is the scenario where SEBE matters most, not least.

**Scenario 4: AI stalls, automation plateaus**
SEBE generates revenue from existing infrastructure (~£15-25B). Still
larger than several existing taxes. Still useful. No harm done.

In every scenario, SEBE is either necessary, useful, or neutral. In no
scenario does it cause harm, provided rates are calibrated correctly
(which is what the consultation and pilot process is for).

## 11. Conclusion

Automation is changing the relationship between production and
employment. Whether this leads to abundance or crisis depends on
whether the gains are distributed or concentrated. The fiscal
architecture determines the outcome.

SEBE provides a tax mechanism that:

- Grows with automation (self-scaling)
- Taxes physical infrastructure (measurable, hard to evade)
- Expands real fiscal space (MMT-compatible)
- Incentivises efficiency and domestic investment (Pigouvian)
- Works across the full range of automation scenarios

The revenue at launch (£34-46B in 2030, 2026 prices) is meaningful.
The growth trajectory (£93B by 2040, £159B by 2045) is substantial.
The mechanism is the contribution. How the revenue is used is a
political choice.

   research

   facilities to validate revenue projections and compliance costs
